Dissertation

Do Primaries Work? Nomination Politics and the Representation of Local Partisan Preferences.

Partisan voters are historically unlikely to cast their votes for the opposing party, so the primary nomination process is perhaps more consequential than ever. Theoretical and empirical scholarship on candidate positioning argues that the primary process is the predominant reason for divergent candidate positions across parties. I argue that the core predictions from the prevailing theory of primaries lacks direct empirical support. Are primary candidate positions are meaningfully related to the preferences of district co-partisans? Do extreme primary constituencies elect extreme primary candidates? I develop Bayesian methods for estimating local partisan preferences and primary candidate positions, and I explore whether the leading view of primaries—and in turn, about the nature of candidate positioning in the US—enjoys empirical support.
