About Me

I grew up near St. Louis, MO, and studied Political Science the University of Missouri. I spent a few summers in Berkeley, CA, where I took some courses while working in a sandwich shop near campus. I moved to Madison for graduate school in 2014.

I study American politics and statistical methodology, with a focus on representation and participation in elections. My dissertation studies local partisan preferences in primary elections. Other projects use new approaches for studying social groups in party coalitions or for understanding and quantifying the effects of voter identification requirements.

Methodologically, I am fascinated by the philosophical reach and practical flexibility of Bayesian methods, which I routinely employ for hierarchical models and measurement models. More recently I am interested in studying causal inference from a Bayesian perspective, where unobserved potential outcomes are treated missing data from a posterior distribution. I try to give each of my projects something methodologically distinctive—innovations on existing ideal point work, a bespoke Bayesian analysis for unconventional data, or a new modeling approach for an old problem.

I am passionate about meta-research problems in political science, particularly at the intersection of academic career incentives and “good science.” I am weary of the field’s reliance on null hypothesis testing for “new theories,” sharp inferences driven by statistical significance, and tenure processes that are hyperfocused on a specific model of research publication. Instead I try to encourage research practices that falsify well-specified alternative models rather than null models, describe findings using the full posterior distribution rather than significance cutoffs, and use open-source practices to improve transparency. I hope for more null findings, uncertain conclusions, “normal science,” and meta-analysis.
